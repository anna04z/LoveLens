# 🔵 Attachment Dimension

## 🌹 S — Secure

**In one sentence:** I can be intimate, and I can be independent. Neither scares me.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

You have a very stable core inside. When love comes, you can invest in it; when love moves a little further away, you don't panic. You don't need constant confirmation, and you don't need to hide yourself for protection. You believe—**a good relationship can make two people both intimate and free at the same time**.

It's not that you have no emotions or never worry—you just **don't get flooded by emotions**. When unease appears, you can see it, and won't immediately let it control your behavior. You know who you are, and you know what you want.

S isn't "perfect" or "no problems"—it's **a basic sense of ease**: trusting that you deserve to be loved, trusting that a relationship can be talked about, repaired, and continued.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Your partner hasn't replied in a few hours** → You think "they must be busy," not fabricating three tragedies
- **There's an uncomfortable conversation** → You can say "what you said earlier felt a bit hurtful, let's talk about it"
- **Your partner is in a bad mood** → You can stay with them, without needing to immediately "fix" them
- **Your partner wants to go out alone with friends** → You sincerely wish them a good time
- **During a smooth stretch** → You enjoy it, you don't secretly worry "is this too smooth?"

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an S-type, the other person feels like they can **finally exhale**—no need to constantly prove love, no need to worry that one wrong word will set off an explosion. You're like gentle but not cold earth, allowing the other person to also be their real self.

Even when you argue, the other person knows **this relationship won't fall apart over one fight**. This sense of ease is one of the rarest things in relationships.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You make love **less effortful**. Many people live their relationships like a test—constantly checking, constantly worrying, constantly scoring. You let the relationship return to what it originally is—**two people together, living comfortably**.

You also give your partner the chance to **learn how to love from you**. A secure partner can often heal a less-secure partner.

### 🌱 Things to notice

Because you're stable inside, you **sometimes underestimate your partner's unease**. When they're anxious, you feel "this isn't a big deal"—but to them, it truly is.

Also, S isn't eternal—**meeting certain people or certain relationships, your anxious or avoidant side can also be activated**. This isn't you "regressing"—everyone has different states. Just keep awareness of yourself.

## 🌸 A — Anxious

**In one sentence:** I long to be loved, but I fear losing.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your love is **hot, deep, and fully invested**. Once you love someone, that person becomes the center of your world—you'll think about what they're doing, what they're thinking, how they're feeling. Your caring is real, but because of that—**you easily feel uneasy**.

A delayed reply, a slightly cooler look, a slightly different phrase—all can stir waves in your heart. It's not that you "think too much"; it's that **your feeling system's sensitivity is simply higher**—you can catch many details others miss. This is your gift, and your weight.

Your deepest fear is: **being left**. So you put in extra effort in the relationship—more expression, more giving, more confirmation. Behind these efforts hides a small voice: "Please don't go."

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Your partner hasn't replied in 30 minutes** → You've already imagined three possibilities
- **When the relationship is going especially smoothly** → You instead worry "is this too good?"
- **After a fight** → You apologize first, even when you're not wrong
- **Your partner wants to be alone tonight** → You say okay on the surface, but inside think "did I do something wrong?"
- **When wanting to express love** → You say it out loud—"I love you," "I miss you," "you look good today"—you don't hold it in

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an A-type, the other person feels **very intense love**—this feeling of being cherished is something many relationships don't have. You make the other person feel they're held in your heart at every moment.

But they **may also feel some pressure**—needing to frequently confirm love, needing to explain every small change, needing to handle your worries. A stable partner can catch these, but not everyone can.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You're not afraid to love. Many people fear depth, fear commitment, fear exposure—you don't. **You're willing to open your heart fully, willing to carry the vulnerability that comes with it**. This is a rare kind of courage.

And your perceptiveness of your partner, your sensitivity to the relationship—these are very precious radar in a relationship. You can sense problems before they grow big.

### 🌱 Things to notice

That "fear of losing" voice is real—but **it's not a fact**. Not every time your partner is cool means they're leaving, not every delayed reply means the relationship is in trouble. **Learn to distinguish "I'm feeling scared" from "things are actually going bad"**—this is a lifelong lesson.

Also, you deserve to be loved—**not because you give a lot**. You've always deserved it. This sentence may need to be said to yourself many times before you slowly believe it.

## 🍃 V — aVoidant

**In one sentence:** I value independence, and I'm wary of being consumed.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your default setting is **I can live well on my own**. It's not that you don't need love—it's that **you don't allow yourself to need love too much**. Because in your feeling system, "needing too much" is dangerous—it makes you lose yourself.

You do love people—it's just that your love isn't the rushing-toward kind. Your love is **keeping a little distance**—through quietly doing things, through steadily being present, through not giving pressure. You appreciate partners who can take care of themselves, partners who don't need you to constantly prove love.

When the relationship gets too close, too frequent, too emotional—you instinctively want to pull back. **It's not that you don't love anymore, you need to breathe**. Give yourself a little space that belongs only to you.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Your partner emotionally wants to talk about the relationship** → You think inside "can we talk later"
- **You've been together too much** → You'll find an excuse to be alone for a bit
- **Your partner needs you to frequently confirm love** → You feel tired, but may not say it directly
- **After a fight** → You'd rather let it pass on its own, don't really want to dig in and talk
- **When asked "what are you thinking"** → You might answer "nothing"—but actually thinking a lot

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a V-type, the other person feels **a quiet, reliable love**—they won't be swept away by emotional storms, won't deal with daily drama, won't feel heavy pressure. You're stable like a tree.

But they **may also feel some loneliness**—especially partners who need verbal confirmation or frequent connection. Your "being there" is real, but if you don't express it, they might not feel it. They'll occasionally wonder: "does this person still love me?"

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You give love **space**. There are too many suffocating relationships in this era—two people tied too tightly, treating each other as everything, losing themselves. You don't do this. You let your partner breathe around you, have their own world, stay a complete self.

You also teach your partner—**love doesn't need to be proven at every moment**. A real love can exist quietly and long.

### 🌱 Things to notice

Between "independence" and "distance" there's a very thin line that you sometimes unconsciously cross. **When you need solitude, tell your partner**—this keeps them from overthinking.

Also, an important thing: **not all withdrawal is self-protection**. Sometimes you retreat because you really need space, but sometimes you're hiding—hiding from deep connection, hiding from commitment, hiding from fully surrendering yourself. **You know in your heart which is which**.

## 🌊 X — disorganiXed

**In one sentence:** I want to get close and want to run—I can't even articulate it myself.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your inside has a **pull**. One side strongly wants to get close—wants to be loved, wants to merge, wants to fully surrender; the other side strongly wants to run—wants to keep distance, wants to protect yourself, wants to be able to pull out anytime. These two impulses **exist simultaneously in you**, often fighting each other.

This isn't you "having problems"—it's that you may have experienced something that made you both love and fear. Maybe a deep but painful relationship, maybe a childhood experience, maybe things you can't even articulate yourself. These traces remain in your feeling system—making you both long for and guard against love.

Your love is **real**. When you invest, you invest deeply; when you withdraw, you withdraw completely. This isn't hypocrisy—there are **truly two forces** in your heart.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **When the relationship is especially intimate** → You suddenly feel suffocated, want distance
- **When distance appears in the relationship** → You start missing them, want to come close again
- **When they ask "what do you actually want"** → You might not be able to answer, because you don't fully know yourself
- **After deeply expressing love** → You may suddenly turn cool, want to retreat
- **During fights** → Emotions might be especially intense, afterwards you're also scared by yourself

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an X-type, the other person feels **very deep love**—when you invest, it's 100% investment, making them feel like the most loved person in the world.

But they **also feel confusion and unease**—because you'll suddenly withdraw, suddenly turn cool, suddenly need space. They'll wonder "did I do something wrong?" But actually **it's not their problem**—it's your own inner pull being activated. A stable partner can catch these, but will need lots of understanding and communication.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

Your love has **real depth**. You don't perform, don't pretend, don't stay on the surface. Every emotion you feel—whether ecstasy or fear—is real. This authenticity itself is rare.

And you're **growing**. If you can see your own patterns (you're seeing them, since you're taking this test), you're already on the way from X to S. **Many disorganized people don't have this awareness their whole lives**. You do.

### 🌱 Things to notice

Your partner **needs you to tell them what's happening to you**—even if you can't articulate it yourself. A sentence like "I'm wanting to pull back right now, but it's not because of you" is a lifeline to them.

Also, **healing takes time**. You won't become secure tomorrow. But you can be a little gentler with yourself every day—when you feel the pull, don't criticize yourself, just watch it and say "oh, here it comes again." This gentle awareness itself is the deepest healing.

## 💬 W — Words

**In one sentence:** I love with words—the kind of love that's spoken.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

You believe—**love that's spoken is love that's alive**. Feelings hidden in the heart, no matter how deep, have limited meaning to the relationship if the other person can't feel them. So you speak—you say "I love you," "I miss you," "thank you," "you look great today," "what you said earlier felt a bit sad."

For you, communication isn't optional—it's **essential oxygen for the relationship**. You don't play guessing games, don't do cold wars, don't make the other person figure it out. **Speak directly, express love directly, discuss hurt directly**—this is your way of loving, and your respect for the relationship.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **When you think of them** → You immediately send a message "just thought of you"
- **When they do something touching** → You'll say it—"what you did really moved me"
- **Sensing the relationship has grown faded** → You'll directly ask "how have we been lately?"—not letting it ferment
- **Sharing daily life with them** → You describe in detail what happened today, what you thought
- **During fights** → You hope to get things clear immediately, don't want to sleep in separate rooms

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a W-type, the other person feels **clear, certain, cared about**—they don't have to guess what's on your mind, you tell them. **This clarity itself is security**.

But they **may occasionally feel information overload**—especially partners who are themselves quiet or need time to settle. They may wish "can we not say so much today, I just want to be quiet."

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You **make love receivable**. Many relationships fail not because there's no love—but because love got hidden, and neither person could feel it. You don't let this kind of loss happen.

Your language also **makes relationships more connected**—late-night long talks, a sentence at a critical moment, comfort when they need it—these words will be remembered by them for a long time.

### 🌱 Things to notice

Not everyone receives love through language. **Your partner may express love through action or presence—you need to learn to see that "unspoken love"**. Them silently remembering your coffee habit is also a kind of "I love you."

Also, sometimes **silence is deeper than speaking**. No need to fill every moment with words—letting some moments just quietly exist is also a kind of intimacy.

## 🛠️ D — Doing

**In one sentence:** I love through actions—the kind of love that can't be seen but can be felt.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

You believe—**love is done, not spoken**. Saying "I love you" is easy, but remembering the other person's preferences every day, quietly preparing dinner when they're tired, taking care of them without saying anything when they're sick—**this is what's real**.

You're not good at sweet talk, but you're **good at turning love into concrete things**. You'll remember what coffee they drink, what food they love, what's bothering them recently, that their shoes are about to wear out, a small wish they mentioned one day. These details others might miss, you remember—then quietly fulfill them.

For you, love isn't performance—it's **those small actions in daily life**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **They're going on a business trip** → You'll pack their bags in advance
- **They're tired** → You may not say anything, but silently prepare dinner
- **Their birthday** → You remember the small thing they casually mentioned wanting
- **Wanting to express love** → You might cook their favorite dish, rather than write a card
- **They're in a bad mood** → You don't say "want to talk," you directly take them somewhere that'll make them happy

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a D-type, the other person feels **cared for**—not controlled, **remembered**. Those tiny things not noticed by most people, you pay attention to. This feeling slowly builds deep groundedness for them.

But they **may occasionally doubt**—because you don't talk much, when you're not doing something, they'll think "do you still love me?" They need a little verbal confirmation.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

Your love is **concrete, verifiable, time-tested**. Sweet words can sound nice but be empty, but the things you've done—each one is solid evidence.

And you give your partner a feeling of **being perfectly caught**—no need to open their mouth to ask, no need to worry, you've already thought of it. This is a precious experience of being loved.

### 🌱 Things to notice

They **also need to hear you say it**. Not everyone can read "I love you" from what you do—especially if they're W-type. **Occasionally open your mouth and say it**—a sentence like "I missed you today" moves them more than doing ten things.

Also, **doing too much can create imbalance**. You keep doing things for them, and they may slowly take it as natural—it's not that they're bad, it's that people in comfort forget. Gently remind yourself: **you also deserve to be cared for**.

## 🌙 P — Presence

**In one sentence:** I love through companionship—not necessarily speaking, but I'm here.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your love is **an art of being there**. You don't need to do many things, don't need to say many words—you believe **"I'm here" itself is the deepest love**.

You like being with them, even when each doing their own thing—watching a movie together, reading together, spacing out together. This "quiet of co-presence" is intimacy to you. You don't need to fill time with activities—**simply being in the same space is beautiful**.

When they're sad, you won't immediately speak or solve problems—you'll sit closer, silently be there, let them know you're here. You believe—**the deepest comfort isn't language, it's not leaving**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **They're in a bad mood** → You sit beside them, don't speak, don't leave
- **Weekends** → You like both being home, each doing your own thing, but in the same space
- **They say "I'm so tired today"** → You don't ask why, you directly sit next to them
- **Long trips** → You enjoy looking out the window together with them, the time of not needing to chat
- **When expressing love** → You might say nothing, just look at them and smile

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a P-type, the other person feels **deeply accompanied**—no pressure, no judgment, no demands. You're like a quiet sea, they can relax leaning against you.

But they **may occasionally feel lonely**—if they're used to receiving love through language or action. Your presence is real, but to those who need "visible evidence," it may not be enough. They'll wonder—"do they actually love me?"

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You teach your partner—**intimacy doesn't need to be performed**. What two people look like when truly at ease is being able to not speak, not do things, just be together. **This quiet presence is a depth many relationships reach for but can't achieve**.

You also give them a **freedom from having to perform**—around you, they don't need to be constantly interesting, constantly fun, constantly "right." Tired is tired, silent is silent. **This is a rare gift**.

### 🌱 Things to notice

Your partner sometimes **needs some signals**—a word, a gesture, a clear expression—to confirm your love is still there. Your "being there" is obvious to you, but they might not be able to read it.

Also, **presence isn't the same as participation**. Sometimes you and your partner are in the same space, but your heart is actually elsewhere—on the phone, on work, in your own thoughts. **Real presence is "the heart is there too"**. Occasionally putting down your phone, looking at them, having a serious chat—is also part of companionship.

## 🏛️ F — Frame

**In one sentence:** I love through structure—the kind of love that seriously manages the relationship.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

You believe—**good relationships don't just naturally happen; they're seriously built by two people**. So you'll talk about rules, expectations, long-term plans, boundaries. These aren't constraints to you—they're **evidence of love**. Because you care that this relationship can go long-term and healthily.

Others may think you're "too rational"—but you know, **reason is your way of protecting love**. You've seen too many relationships ruined by "I thought you understood," "I thought we'd naturally be fine"—you don't want that kind of regret to happen to you. So you're willing to spend time talking clearly.

Your love is **not just feeling, but also commitment and maintenance**. You treat the relationship as a joint project for two people—taking it seriously, regularly reviewing, actively adjusting. It doesn't sound romantic, but **this is a very deep kind of romance**—because you're truly seriously loving.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Early in the relationship** → You want to discuss "our expectations for this relationship"
- **Wanting to express love** → You'll plan their birthday very thoughtfully and completely
- **When disagreements arise** → You'll say "let's set a time to talk this through"
- **Caring about their future** → You'll ask "where do you want to be in three years?"
- **Facing stress** → You'll make plan A, plan B, plan C

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an F-type, the other person feels **very grounded**—you're not the "drifting" kind of partner; you're the serious, planning, reliable kind. Being with you, the relationship won't inexplicably fall apart—**because you won't let it fall apart that way**.

But they **may occasionally feel breathless**—if they're more emotional or spontaneity-loving. Your "seriousness" might feel like "sternness" to them. They'll wonder—"can we not plan every single thing?"

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You let the relationship **go long and healthy**. Many relationships lose to "natural attrition"—not that love faded, but no one maintained it. You don't let this happen. You're willing to put in the effort, treating this relationship as something worth investing in.

You also give your partner a **sense of direction**—knowing where you're going, what you're working toward, what you're planning. This sense of direction itself is security.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**Sometimes love doesn't go according to plan**. Those unannounced hugs, sudden longings, unexplained "I want to see you today" moments—**these are important too**. Don't let your planning override them.

Also, **not every problem needs to be "talked through"**. Some emotions need empathy, not analysis. **Sometimes they just want to be held, not hear you say "I think the essence of this matter is..."**

## 🌸 M — Merging

**In one sentence:** I want to become one with you.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your ideal relationship is—**two people becoming one person**. Sharing daily life, sharing emotions, sharing life, sharing decisions. There's no "your thing" or "my thing"—only "our thing."

You're not afraid of this closeness—**you enjoy it**. That feeling of not being able to tell yourselves apart, that feeling of melting into each other, that feeling of "without them I'm not whole"—to you, this is love's most beautiful form.

You want your partner to **be the center of your world**. Living together, making decisions together, planning the future together, even picking clothes together. Others may think this loses the self—but **you're freely choosing this merging**. To you, oneness isn't loss, it's **finding the other half of yourself**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Ideal weekend** → Two people together from morning to night
- **Making important decisions** → You hope your partner is involved from beginning to end
- **Partner has a hobby you don't know** → You want to learn about it together
- **The word "we"** → You use it more and more to replace "I" and "you"
- **Not seeing each other for a while** → You miss every moment of them

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an M-type, the other person feels **intense love and investment**—you make them feel like the center of your world, this feeling of being cherished is very strong.

But they **may also feel some pressure**—especially partners who need independent space. They might think "I need some time to myself" but fear upsetting you. A partner who also leans toward merging would be very suitable for you, but **N or L type partners need you to understand their rhythm**.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You're willing to **fully invest yourself**. In this era when everyone "holds back a bit of themselves," your wholehearted investment is rare. You make your partner feel fully chosen, fully loved, without reservation.

You also create **a possibility for deep connection**—two souls merged as one can reach depths that many other relationships can't.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**Merging doesn't equal healthy**. If you treat them as your entire world, once they're not there (business trip, busy, wanting solitude), you'll be very lost. **Keep some things for yourself**—friends, hobbies, goals—isn't betrayal of love, it's protection of yourself.

Also, **they need air too**. When they say "I want to be alone for a bit," it's not not loving you—it's charging. Let them finish charging and come back, they'll love you more.

## 🌿 N — Nested

**In one sentence:** We have overlap, and we have our own space.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your ideal relationship is—**two independent people, with a common overlap**. You have your life, I have my life, but **our lives have an overlapping part**—that's "us."

You don't want to merge—that would suffocate; you don't want to be completely parallel—that would be distant. What you want is **that middle part**—both intimate and independent, connected and free.

You value shared experiences, shared friends, shared daily life—but also respect that they have their own friend circle, interests, growth rhythm. **You believe the meeting of two complete people has more depth than the merging of two incomplete people**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Ideal weekend** → Most of the time together, but leaving a little time for yourself
- **Making important decisions** → Tell each other before deciding, listen to opinions, but not all details are co-decided
- **Partner has a hobby you don't know** → You respect and roughly understand, but don't necessarily participate
- **The word "we"** → Naturally appears alongside "I" and "you"
- **Long business trips** → You miss them but don't panic, and know they're doing well

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an N-type, the other person feels **a comfortable balance**—both loved and respected as an independent person. This relationship is **the most ideal form** to many people—not suffocating, not distant, just right.

But they **may occasionally need more**—more merging (M-type partners) or more space (L/O-type partners). You can adjust between the two ends, but your basic "nested" principle you'll maintain.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You let both people **stay whole**. In many relationships someone slowly loses themselves—you won't. You and your partner can both grow into more complete selves in this relationship, rather than becoming "other halves."

Your relationship is also **more sustainable**—two complete people don't deplete each other, two people with their own worlds can nourish each other.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**The art of boundaries takes practice**. You know both "we" and "I" matter, but where exactly is the ratio? When to get close, when to pull back?—this needs to be figured out with your partner.

Also, **occasionally allow yourself to merge a bit more**. Not every closeness is a threat—sometimes letting the boundaries blur a little, you'll discover a new kind of sweetness.

## 🏛️ L — paraLlel

**In one sentence:** We each walk our own paths, side by side.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your ideal relationship is—**two independent people, walking side by side**. Not merging, not just having overlap—**each walking their own path, but in the same direction**.

You appreciate partners with their own world—their own career, friends, interests, growth rhythm. Two people being together isn't because of "filling each other in"—it's because **"we're each complete, but chose to be together"**.

You don't mind your partner having life areas you don't know about—you even find **this pretty cool**. You don't need to know every one of each other's things, don't need to make every decision together—but you know each other is there, know you're going in the same big direction.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Ideal weekend** → Each doing own things during the day, together at night
- **Making important decisions** → Decide first then tell your partner, seek opinions but don't wait for their nod
- **Partner has a friend circle you don't know** → You think it's great, no need to join
- **The word "we"** → Only used when talking about common things, otherwise "I" and "you"
- **Communication frequency** → Talk when there's something, don't have to if there isn't

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an L-type, the other person feels **great freedom**—not monitored, not demanded of, not merged with. This relationship is ideal for very independent people—**finally someone who understands I'm not clingy**.

But they **may also feel some distance**—if they need more connection. They'll wonder "how much 'we' is there between us?" A partner who also leans L would be very suitable, but **M-type partners would need adjustment**.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You make love **light**. The relationship isn't a burden, isn't constraint, isn't mutual restraint—it's companionship freely chosen by two free people. **This relationship form is very mature**—it needs both people to have strong selves.

Your partner always knows—**their freedom is respected**. This respect itself is deep love.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**If parallel is too parallel, the two lines never meet**. Sometimes you need to consciously create intersections—shared experiences, shared memories, shared moments. Otherwise slowly, "side by side" becomes "roommates living together."

Also, **your partner needs to know you're in "us"**. Because you don't constantly give them a sense of communality like M or N does, you need to tell them in other ways—"I chose you, I keep choosing."

## 🌌 O — Orbit

**In one sentence:** I'm in your life, but I keep my distance.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your ideal relationship is—**I'm at the edge of your world, you're at the edge of mine**. Not becoming one, not having big overlap, not just parallel either—**keeping a distance where you can see each other but not touch**.

You love the other person, but you don't want love to **take up too much of your life**. You need your own time, space, orbit. It's best if they're the same. You two are like stars each in their own orbit—**you'll meet, but won't collide**.

This isn't coldness—**it's a very deep self-protection**. You may have experienced moments of "being swallowed by love," so you learned **to love from afar**. This way lets you both be in the relationship and keep yourself.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Ideal weekend** → See each other if wanted, otherwise each on your own
- **Making important decisions** → Unless it affects them, this is your own business
- **Partner has their own circle** → You have no need to join, that's their world
- **Communication frequency** → Talk when you think of it, let it pass when you don't
- **Long business trips** → You enjoy this alone time

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an O-type, the other person feels **enormous respect**—you don't interfere, don't probe, don't possess. This relationship is a relief for very independent people—**finally someone who doesn't cling to me**.

But they **may also feel lonely**—if they expect more "us." They'll wonder "what position do I have in your life?" A partner who also leans O would match you very well, but **M or N-type partners would feel too far**.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

Your love is **the distance of stars**—far, but always shining. This love won't mutually deplete, won't mutually swallow, won't mutually limit. **In an era flooded with suffocating relationships, your love is a clear stream**.

You also let your partner understand—**love doesn't need to be proven every moment**. You two contact each other rarely, but love is still there. This needs both people to have very deep inner security.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**Between O and coldness is just a line**. You know in your heart which distances are self-protection and which are withdrawing from this relationship. **Honestly face this distinction**—some "orbits" are actually quiet goodbyes.

Also, **they need occasional signals**—telling them you're still there, still love, still care. You don't need to change your rhythm, but an occasional "I missed you" means a lot to them.

## ⚔️ C — Confront

**In one sentence:** If there's a problem, let's talk—the sooner the better.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your default mode for handling conflict is—**face it, directly, immediately**. You can't stand letting things ferment, can't stand cold wars, can't stand "let's pretend nothing's wrong." To you, **putting words on the table is how problems get solved**—hidden problems just slowly mold.

You believe—**a relationship's health isn't about having no conflict; it's about being able to talk about conflict well**. So you're willing to open your mouth first, willing to bear the other person's emotional reaction, willing to endure the discomfort during the conversation—**because you know after the talk, the relationship will be better**.

You don't love fighting—you're **willing to put in the effort of talking for the relationship**. Behind this is a deep caring.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Feeling something is off** → You'll immediately say "let's talk"
- **Your partner said something that made you uncomfortable** → You point it out right there
- **After a fight** → You want to reconcile immediately, talk it through immediately
- **Accumulated grievances** → You'll find a time to say it all at once
- **They don't want to talk** → You'll gently but persistently—"I know you need time, but we're talking tonight"

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a C-type, the other person feels **no uncertainty**—any problem will be talked out, there won't be undercurrents. **This clarity itself is security**.

But they **may also feel pressure**—especially partners who need digestion time (T-types). They'll feel "can you let me think first before we talk?" A partner who also leans C is a very good match, but **T-type partners need you to learn to wait**.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You give the relationship **real room for growth**. Many relationships slowly wither in "pretending nothing's wrong"—both people know there's a problem, but neither speaks, and in the end love gets worn away. **You don't let this happen**.

Your directness also lets them know—**you really care about this relationship**. Being willing to talk is a kind of commitment.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**There's a line between "confronting" and "attacking"**. You can speak the problem directly—but carry the intention of repair, not the intention of hurt. "What you said earlier made me uncomfortable" is much healthier than "why are you always like this?"

Also, **not every problem needs to be talked about immediately**. Some things discussed in the heat of emotion get worse—**waiting isn't avoidance, it's choosing a better moment**.

## 🧠 T — Think

**In one sentence:** I need time to think about it on my own.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your default mode for handling conflict is—**pull back first, digest alone**. When conflict happens, your first reaction isn't to open your mouth—it's **"let me think about it first"**. You need time to see clearly what you're feeling, what this thing really means, how you want to respond.

This isn't avoidance—**it's deep processing**. You don't want to speak within emotions, because words spoken within emotions might not be what you really want to say. You'd rather wait until you're calm, have thought clearly, have translated complex emotions into clear language—**and only then open your mouth**.

In relationships you're the **"slow but steady"** side. You won't be impulsive, won't overreact, won't say words you'll regret. But this also means—**sometimes your partner waits a long time for you**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Conflict just happened** → You go silent, need to be alone
- **They ask "what do you think"** → You say "let me think more"
- **After a fight** → You may need a night or even days to digest
- **Being rushed to respond** → You feel very pressured, but also don't want to speak without thinking
- **After thinking clearly** → What you say will be very clear, very on point, very worth hearing

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a T-type, the other person feels **taken seriously**—you won't respond carelessly, won't be emotional, won't say things you'll take back. **Every word you say has been thought about**.

But they **may also feel anxious**—especially C-type partners. Your silence feels to them like "I'm being pushed away." You need to tell them—"I'm thinking, not moving away from you."

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

Your response has **depth, weight**. Many conflicts happen because both sides are in emotion poking at each other—you break this cycle. Your thinking keeps problems from adding fuel to fire.

You also give your partner a **demonstration**—not all things need immediate reaction. Sometimes being silent for a bit is more loving than speaking immediately.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**There's a line between "thinking" and "avoiding"**. Sometimes you think you're thinking—but you're actually delaying. You know in your heart which is which.

Also, **tell them you need time**. Your silence in your eyes is "I'm processing," but in theirs might be "no longer loving." A sentence like "I need 24 hours to think, then we'll talk" can avoid many misunderstandings.

## 🌿 R — Repair

**In one sentence:** First let the atmosphere get better, then slowly talk.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your default mode for handling conflict is—**first repair the atmosphere, then handle the problem**. You don't like tension, don't like cold wars, don't like letting things stay uncomfortable. So you'll do something—make a meal, send a joke, hand water, actively hug—**let the atmosphere ease first**.

You believe—**people can't talk things out well in tension**. Both in anger, defensive, feeling attacked by each other—in this state nothing said works. **First let each other return to the state of "we're together"**, then discuss the specific problem.

You're the **gentle mediator** in the relationship. Not that you don't discuss problems—you just know **the timing for discussing problems matters**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Conflict is intense** → You actively change the subject, interrupt, make a joke
- **In cold war** → You'll make their favorite food, or send a light message
- **Their mood is bad** → You let them feel loved first, then ask what's up
- **After a fight** → You actively extend your hand, actively hug, actively reconcile
- **Long-term friction** → You use daily gentleness to accumulate goodwill, letting problems fade on their own

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With an R-type, the other person feels **the relationship always has a way back**—no matter how fierce the fight, there's always someone who extends their hand first. **This gentle mediation makes the relationship hard to truly break**.

But they **may also feel some problems haven't been truly solved**—because you tend to let the atmosphere get good first, but sometimes after it gets good, **that problem also doesn't get discussed anymore**. C-type partners would want more directness.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You make the relationship **resilient**. Fighting is part of every relationship—but whether someone is willing to actively repair is key to whether the relationship lasts. **You're that person**.

Your repair also lets your partner understand—**even in conflict, you still love each other**. This belief slowly becomes the base color of the relationship.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**Repairing the atmosphere isn't the same as solving the problem**. Some things need to be discussed even after the atmosphere is good—otherwise they'll continue existing in more hidden ways, eventually becoming "old wounds" in the relationship.

Also, **you may make them feel "arguing is useless"**—because every fight gets gently dissolved by you, no one discusses the specific problems. Occasionally let yourself not repair, let them take initiative once—**this is also a healthy balance**.

## 🌾 Y — Yield

**In one sentence:** Let it go, the relationship matters more than being right.

### 🌿 What kind of person this is

Your default mode for handling conflict is—**step back**. Not that you have no opinion, not that you don't care—you **value the relationship more than personal right and wrong**. In your heart, rather than winning a fight and losing the warmth—it's better to yield a step and preserve this relationship.

You believe—**many things don't need to be talked through**. Time will make them fade, love will make them unimportant. Continuing to get tangled has no meaning—**better to put it down, move forward**.

You're not cowardly—you've **chosen a relationship-first philosophy**. This needs maturity, and confidence in your own caring—**you're not afraid of losing the fight, you just feel winning the fight is also meaningless**.

### 📍 What it looks like in daily life

- **Different opinions** → You'll say "okay, let's do what you say"
- **They did something uncomfortable for you** → You'll think "forget it, avoid it next time"
- **Long-term small dissatisfactions** → You choose to keep letting them be, not specifically bringing them up
- **Mid-fight** → You'll pause first "okay, you're right"
- **Yielding on important things** → You'll comfort yourself "the relationship matters more than this"

### 💭 How it feels to be with you

With a Y-type, the other person feels **the relationship has no gunpowder smell**—not fighting over every small thing, not arguing for face, not bringing up old grievances. **This gentleness makes the relationship very comfortable**.

But they **may also feel confusion**—what exactly do you want? Because you don't really express dissatisfaction, they may not know what you mind, what you like, what you need. Long-term, they may **unintentionally cross your boundaries**, because they don't know those are your boundaries.

### ✨ The beauty of this way of loving

You save the relationship **lots of exhaustion**. Many couples spend energy on a pile of small things competing with each other—you don't. You choose to save energy for truly important things. **This is a wisdom**.

You also make your partner feel **the acceptance of being loved**—you're not nitpicking their every small flaw, you're willing to accept them as they are. This acceptance itself is deep love.

### 🌱 Things to notice

**If you yield too much, you become disappearance**. Yielding a little each time—slowly you'll get smaller, lose yourself, become someone who doesn't dare mention needs. **Some things are worth yielding on, some aren't**. Learn to distinguish.

Also, **not expressing needs isn't the same as not having needs**. Your needs are still there—just hidden. They'll slowly accumulate, one day erupting in ways you didn't expect—or making you quietly exit this relationship. **Gently telling them what you want isn't asking; it's making the relationship more real**.
